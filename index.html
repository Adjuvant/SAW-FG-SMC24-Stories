Gabrielle's Open-plan Office Scenarios

Situation and Noise Context

Our stories centre around Gabrielle, a mid-20s programmer at a software agency. At work, she organises work plans, creates code, and collaborates well with clients. Gabrielle work is busy, many deadlines across different projects. In this churn of tasks, office distractions are a challenge for her. But she strives to create a positive work environment for everyone. The main thing she wants is for the team to work together better, listen to each other's ideas, and support each other in finding solutions.

The open-plan office is usually a bustling hub of activity and energy with people coming and going throughout the day. The hum of conversation and work fill the air. The scent of coffee wafts from the kitchen area. Desks sit in neat rows, and large windows allow natural light to flood the space. Monitors glow with the bright colours of screensavers and streams of emails.

The hustle and bustle of a busy open-plan office isn't Gabrielle’s favourite thing. She's surrounded by the sounds of clacking keyboards, distant conversations, laughter, and sneezing. For her, loud conversations nearby and music can be distracting when she's trying to concentrate. Although it can be overwhelming at times, she has learned to tune out the noise and focus on her work.

Sound Annotation Utopia

This week HR decided to provide us all with a sound analyser device as part of a wellbeing initiative. HR says that once they have collected data from everyone, they can start looking at new policies for sound etiquette in the office. I was curious how this would work out.

Today, I tested out the new sound tagging device. It was a handheld device that allowed us to label sounds. By pointing it at a sound and pressing a button, I could label it as either annoying or pleasant.

I started off by tagging the sound of the coffee machine, which was a comforting sound for me, one that reminded me of the start of a new day. The hum of the air conditioner was also a pleasant sound, providing a constant background noise that seemed to keep me focused. As I made my way around the office, I heard a printer jamming. It was an irritating sound, so I quickly pointed the device at the printer and tagged it. But the sound of my co-worker typing on their keyboard was rhythmic and soothing. As I walked around further, I heard someone chewing and chomping. I tagged the sound as annoying and made a mental note to avoid sitting near them during lunch.

Though it was uncomfortable at first, I started enjoying tagging things and became more aware of the sounds around me. It helped me appreciate sounds in a new way and feel more productive. I now feel more connected to my surroundings and appreciate the little things that make everyday life special.

Once HR had all the data they wanted, we gave back the analyser devices, which was a shame as I was still enjoying my little sound walks. A week later, tech support installed monitoring microphones in the office, and we had a live visualisation of the office sound map. It showed us all the good and bad parts of the office for different activities. If you wanted to concentrate, the corner away from the meeting rooms was best. If you wanted to brainstorm, the spaces by the lounge would cause the least disturbance to others.

At first, we were all self-conscious and tiptoed around the office, afraid to disturb the sound map. But as we got used to it and experimented, we found that kindness worked best. The system showed when we were polite, encouraging us to listen and respond with empathy. We had more meaningful conversations, sharing stories without judgment or interruption. As a result, the office became a place of connection and collaboration, where everyone felt seen and heard. Together, we solved problems, brainstormed new ideas, and created something beautiful.

Sound Annotation Dystopia

Given recent reports of noise disturbance in the office, HR decided that something had to change, so they are trialling a new AI sound analyser service. They say that once it’s trained, they can make new noise etiquette rules about what we can do where, and when, in the office.

So, HR assigned me a new task: testing a sound analyser device. It’s a box with a bunch of microphones and buttons on it to label sounds as either annoying or pleasant. After going through the obscure onboarding to position it in the room and calibrate its microphones, it was ready to go. The how-to says that you should use it when you notice a sound as either pleasant or annoying. But if you miss something the system is monitoring for novel sounds and will request an office user for a label.

So now the box tells when it wants me to label something in the web app. Every so often I’m in the middle of coding and BING! the device lights up and I get a notification on my screen. The analyser has decided I need to listen to something and give it a label. I get it, it’s my task, but it is disrupting. Sometimes it takes ages to recover from such changes of context.

Using it has surprised me in some ways. I've noticed how much I hate the sound of my co-worker eating. Although there are sounds I enjoy, I tend to forget to use it when I hear them. Instead, I only seem to reach for it when I'm annoyed by something. Also, I can't help but feel like something has changed. I'm no longer listening to the sounds of the office; I'm listening to something else. A judgement, a classification, a label.

Once HR had all the data they wanted, we gave back the analyser devices. A week later, tech support installed monitoring microphones and LED fill lights throughout the office. The service uses these to create a live visualisation of the office sound map, showing all the areas with good and bad noise spots. Like a live colour map overlayed in the office.

The sound analyser service did more than check noise. It also provided feedback on our tone and choice of words, acting like a judgmental co-worker critiquing every utterance. This made me second-guess every sentence, speaking more cautiously to avoid criticism from the analyser’s LED indicators. But even that was not enough, as it sometimes criticised me for being too quiet or too loud. It felt like trying to please a shifting, invisible audience.

The sound analyser changed the office atmosphere and made us uncomfortable. We felt judged, making us on edge. Office dynamics changed from a comfortable space to a place of tension. I don't know if the Sound Analyzer service intended to do that, to change the way we communicated with each other. But that's what it did. And now, I still feel its presence, still feeling the judgmental microphone hovering over me, waiting for me to say the wrong thing.

Sound Controller Utopia

So, I heard about this new AI sound system from a colleague and decided to give it a shot. The AI listens and changes the soundscape based on your preferences. Pretty cool, huh?

It's a subscription service where you rent their special equipment. So, an engineer came to install special speakers on my desk and set me up with the training software. The software put me through a pile of sound examples and asked me to tag them as pleasant or annoying to tune into my preferences. Once trained, I could choose from a range of ‘moods’ via an app, including focus mode, relaxing, and social.

At first, I was a bit sceptical. Could an AI change how I perceive the world? Then something strange began to happen.

The sound of waves lapping against the shore filled the reception area, creating a sense of calm. The AI sent live sound from real places, like beaches and rainforests, into public spaces. It was perfect for those who craved a little bit of nature in their workday. A cool feature was it having different settings for times of the day. During lunch breaks, the sounds of a bustling city street jostled. In the evening a gentle river and birdsong calmed those working late. This made you feel like there were little episodes to the day and you felt as is a story was unfolding.

At my desk, the sounds of the office began to change. When someone began to eat, and my annoyance rose, a low fuzzy drone slowly began to fill the air, just right to block out the sound of the chewing. When someone tried to play music, the sound faded away to an inaudible whisper. The AI sound system subtly adjusted to my environment, responding to my mood, and created a sonic atmosphere that I was comfortable with. This made me feel a sense of control and focus that I didn't have before.

After fiddling with the profile system some more, it turns out I could make new ‘moods’. So, I created a special soundscape for when I was brainstorming. It mixed the sounds of a forest from my childhood, the chatter of a coffee shop, and the distant buzz of a plane taking off. For some reason, this really worked when I was trying to come up with ideas. During a group brainstorming session, something unusual happened. I arrived early to the meeting room and logged into my profile to use the sound system and projector. But, once we started working, a co-worker started complaining that they couldn't focus and began questioning the strange soundscape. It’s cool, everyone is different, so we decided to turn it off and continue our work without it.

Although the experience was initially disconcerting, I soon began to enjoy it. When I move from my desk, the AI sound system fades away, and the familiar sounds of the office surround me once again. But I'm happy in the knowledge that my little corner of the office could be just the way I like it.

Sound Controller Dystopia

While I always found working in the office a little distracting, I had yet to realise how bad it could get.

While working at my computer, I heard an odd sound in the air. Confused, I glanced around, but my colleagues were oblivious. I removed my headphones, but the sound wasn't coming from them. The sound stopped, and I went back to work. But then another sound disrupted me -- the sound of someone eating. It was coming from the other side of the office, yet the noise seemed so close. At lunch in the staff room, it seemed like I could hear rainforest birds chirping, so I stuck in my headphones. Then, as I walked through reception to leave for the day, I heard waves crashing against the shore. It was nice, but I also began to hear conversations in the background, as if they were coming from a real place.

As time went on, I noticed other strange things. Music would play that I didn't like, and conversations would be louder than normal. It was as if something was playing a cruel joke on me, trying to make me feel uncomfortable in my own workplace. I went to HR to find out what was going on.

The office got a new sound system that uses AI to change the sounds it makes depending on what each person likes. There was also a nature sound system installed in the public areas. HR sent a group email informing us, but I had missed it while on holiday. This might explain why there were so many weird sounds around me.

To dig a bit deeper, I signed up for the AI service and reviewed the FAQ. I found that when the AI system lacks a user profile, it attempts to establish one through trial and error. This is what it had been doing with me. To know my preferences, it experimented on me. It noted my likes and dislikes through facial and behavioural recognition. So, I reset my profile, suffered through two hours of tagging noises in their web platform. Finally, I selected my preferences - nature sounds and no music for concentration. For a while, it worked well. The office music softened to whisper, and eating noises blurred into a low frequency mush. Monkeys and birds still battled in the staff room, which, amusing at first, soon became distracting.

But I started to notice other strange things about the sound system. Music played that I didn't like, and colleague's Zoom chats boomed out. I tried to drown it out with my nature sounds, but it kept making these weird errors.

A customer support chatbot explained that the sound system blended my preferences with those of my desk neighbour due to the special speaker placement. Uncomfortable and distracted with this fuzzy blend, I removed my consent and data from the platform. System tech support came in, altered the speaker arrangement. I took back control.

Back in my normal chaos, I found relief. I stuck on my noise-cancelling headphones and focused on my work. I learned that the simplest solution can often be the best one.

Darius' Work From Home Scenarios

Situation and Noise Context

Our stories centre on Darius, a mid-30s city planner and designer working at an architecture consultancy. He lives in a small but comfy two-story house in the Bow region of London with his partner and young daughter. Darius works from home but occasionally attends studio meetings and site visits around the country. He spends his time organising work plans, creating visualisations, and collaborating with clients.

Each morning, Darius wakes up early to enjoy a cup of coffee before settling down at his desk. He looks out the window and takes in the sights and sounds of the city. When his daughter is ready for playgroup, Darius walks her there. During his workday, Darius takes walking breaks and occasionally works at a nearby café. He has a system and schedule that works for him and his family and is committed to finding the right balance between work and home life. Dedicating quality time with his daughter every day, Darius is confident he can maintain a comfortable and productive home office environment.

Darius has set up a home office that enables him to focus and be productive. His office area is in the spare bedroom next to the living room, it is also a place for guests to stay and additional storage. The office is equipped with all the necessary tools and equipment, including a computer, drawing tablet, assorted office equipment, and document systems. The normal distractions are his daughter playing in the living room and the neighbour's dog barking. However, recently construction work has started on the neighbour's basement conversion, resulting in significant noise issues due to drilling, workmen, and other machine noise.
